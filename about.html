The Beginning

A restaurant born from
a backyard fire pit

EMBER didn't start in a commercial kitchen. It started in a backyard in Southeast Portland, around a fire pit that Chef Marcus Hale built from reclaimed brick.

Every weekend, friends and neighbors would gather. Marcus would cook whatever the farmers' market offered that morning: whole trout wrapped in grape leaves, stone fruit charred until the sugars caramelized, bread dough pressed against heated cast iron. No recipes. No plan. Just fire, ingredients, and intuition.

Word spread. The weekend gatherings grew from six people to sixty. In the spring of 2019, Marcus signed a lease on a century-old building in the Pearl District and spent eighteen months converting it into the restaurant he'd been rehearsing for years: a 40-seat dining room built around a custom open hearth that burns Oregon white oak, fruitwood, and seasonal hardwoods.

EMBER opened its doors in October 2020. The philosophy hasn't changed since those backyard dinners: cook what the land gives you, let fire do the work, and keep it honest.

The Chef

Marcus Hale

Executive Chef & Founder

Marcus spent a decade cooking in kitchens that ranged from a Michelin-starred restaurant in Lyon to a seafood shack on the Oregon coast. Each experience stripped away something unnecessary and reinforced one conviction: the best food comes from restraint, not complexity.

His approach at EMBER is deceptively simple. Source the best ingredients within 100 miles. Use fire as the primary cooking method. Season with precision. Plate with intention. Let the ingredient be the star.

In 2025, EMBER earned its first Michelin star. Marcus was named a James Beard Award semifinalist for Best Chef: Northwest. He doesn't talk about either much. He'd rather talk about the farmer in Canby who grows the heirloom tomatoes, or the forager in the Cascades who finds the chanterelles.

"Fire doesn't lie. You can't hide behind technique when cooking over an open flame. Every ingredient has to earn its place on the plate."

Our Principles

Three rules we cook by

01

Fire First

Every dish on our menu touches live flame. We don't own a microwave, a sous vide circulator, or a deep fryer. Our kitchen is built around a six-foot open hearth, a wood-fired oven, and a charcoal grill. If it can't be cooked over fire, it doesn't belong here.

02

Within 100 Miles

We source 90% of our ingredients from farms, ranches, and foragers within 100 miles of our kitchen. The menu changes every week because the land changes every week. We don't fight seasons. We follow them.

03

Nothing Wasted

Vegetable trimmings become stocks and ferments. Bones are roasted for sauces. Bread that doesn't sell becomes croutons or bread crumbs. Hardwood ash seasons our butter. We aim for zero waste because restraint makes you more creative, not less.

The Space

40 seats. One hearth.
No distractions.

Our dining room is designed so every seat has a view of the open hearth. The space is intimate on purpose: 40 seats means we can give each table the attention it deserves. Reclaimed Douglas fir, hand-forged iron fixtures, and candlelight set the tone. The music stays low. The focus stays on the food and the people you're sharing it with.

Our Sources

We know every farmer by name

Our produce comes from Groundwork Organics in Junction City. Our beef is raised on pasture at Carman Ranch in Wallowa County. Our mushrooms are foraged in the Cascades by a forager named Jim who's been walking those woods for thirty years.

We don't use the word "farm-to-table" because it's become meaningless. Instead, we'll just tell you: the tomato on your plate was picked yesterday morning, driven up from Canby, and roasted over oak coals this afternoon. That's the supply chain. There's nothing between the field and your fork except fire.
